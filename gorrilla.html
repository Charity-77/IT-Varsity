GORRILLA

Gorillas are large primates of the genus Gorilla and are part of the family Hominidae. Two species are traditionally recognised: the eastern gorilla (Gorilla beringei) and the western gorilla (Gorilla gorilla), with each species further divided into subspecies such as the mountain gorilla and the western lowland gorilla. Gorillas are found in the tropical and subtropical forests of central Africa. They are the largest living primates and share a significant percentage of their DNA with humans, making them one of our closest relatives.

All gorillas have several distinctive features, the most notable of which is their large, muscular build and broad chests. They walk using their knuckles and have opposable thumbs and toes that aid in grasping. Adult males develop a patch of silver hair on their backs, earning them the name “silverbacks.” Gorillas have a gentle nature, communicate through vocalizations and gestures, and live in social groups led by a dominant silverback. Their expressive faces and complex emotions are similar to humans.

Gorillas are herbivorous and primarily eat leaves, stems, fruit, and bamboo shoots. They inhabit dense forests, including lowland swamps and montane forests, and are usually found near areas with abundant vegetation and water sources.
