GIRAFFE

Giraffes are tall mammals belonging to the family Giraffidae and the order Artiodactyla. One extant species is recognised, the giraffe (Giraffa camelopardalis), although several subspecies exist, including the Masai giraffe, Reticulated giraffe, and Southern giraffe. Some recent genetic studies suggest that these may represent separate species. Giraffes are native to various regions of sub-Saharan Africa, typically found in savannahs, grasslands, and open woodlands. Giraffidae also includes the okapi, the giraffe's only living relative. Giraffes are the tallest terrestrial animals, with adult males reaching heights up to 5.5 m (18 ft) and weighing around 1,200 kg (2,600 lb).

All giraffes have several distinctive features, the most notable of which is their long neck, which allows them to reach foliage high in trees that other herbivores cannot access. They also have long legs and a unique coat pattern consisting of patches separated by lighter lines, which provides camouflage in their environment. Their large eyes give them excellent vision, and their long prehensile tongues, measuring up to 45 cm (18 in), assist in grasping leaves and twigs. Despite their height, giraffes have only seven neck vertebrae, the same number as most mammals.

Giraffes are herbivorous and primarily feed on the leaves, flowers, and fruits of trees and shrubs, particularly acacia species. They are typically found in areas with abundant browse and need to drink regularly, so they often stay near water sources.
