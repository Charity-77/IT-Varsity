KOALA

Koalas are small to medium-sized marsupials of the family Phascolarctidae and the order Diprotodontia. A single extant species is recognised, the koala (Phascolarctos cinereus), which is native to Australia. Although they are sometimes called "koala bears", they are not bears but marsupials. Koalas are found in coastal regions of eastern and southern Australia, from Queensland to South Australia. Phascolarctidae is a family that once included several extinct relatives of the koala, but today only the koala survives. Adult male koalas can reach a length of 85 cm (33 in) and weigh up to 14 kg (31 lb).

All koalas have several distinctive features, the most notable of which is their large, leathery nose, used for detecting scent and recognizing other koalas. They have strong limbs and sharp claws adapted for climbing trees. Koalas have a thick, soft coat of fur and large, fluffy ears that enhance their sense of hearing. Their opposable digits and rough pads help them grip tree branches. Koalas possess a highly specialized digestive system, including a long cecum, allowing them to digest toxic eucalyptus leaves, which make up the bulk of their diet.
