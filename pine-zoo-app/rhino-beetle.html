RHINO-BEETLE

Rhino beetles are large insects belonging to the subfamily Dynastinae, within the family Scarabaeidae and the order Coleoptera. Many species of rhino beetles are recognized, including the European rhinoceros beetle (Oryctes nasicornis) and the Japanese rhinoceros beetle (Allomyrina dichotoma). Some scientists classify other related beetles, such as the Hercules beetle and the elephant beetle, under the broader rhinoceros beetle group due to shared characteristics. Rhino beetles are distributed across tropical and subtropical regions including parts of Asia, Africa, the Americas, and Oceania. Dynastinae is a diverse and still extant subfamily of scarab beetles; other related but distinct groups include dung beetles, flower chafers, and June beetles. Male rhino beetles are among the strongest insects and can lift objects up to 850 times their body weight.

All rhino beetles possess several distinctive features, the most prominent being a large horn-like structure on the malesâ€™ head or thorax, used mainly for combat and mating displays. These horns vary in shape and size depending on the species. Rhino beetles have a hard exoskeleton that offers protection, and they use their strong forelegs to dig and climb. Their elytra (hardened forewings) protect the delicate flight wings underneath. Males typically have more developed horns than females, and while they appear fearsome, rhino beetles are harmless to humans.

Rhino beetles are herbivorous and commonly found in habitats such as forests, gardens, and agricultural areas. They are especially attracted to decaying wood, fruit, and sap, and many species thrive in warm, humid environments.
