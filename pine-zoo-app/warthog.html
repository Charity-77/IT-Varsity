WARTHOG

Warthogs are wild members of the pig family Suidae and the order Artiodactyla. Two species are commonly recognised: the common warthog (Phacochoerus africanus) and the desert warthog (Phacochoerus aethiopicus), although the latter is less widespread and more specialized for arid environments. Warthogs are distributed across much of sub-Saharan Africa, particularly in savannahs, grasslands, and lightly wooded areas. Suidae includes other wild pigs such as bushpigs and red river hogs, but warthogs are among the most adapted for open terrain. Male common warthogs can reach a shoulder height of up to 85 cm (33 in) and weigh as much as 150 kg (330 lb).

All warthogs have several distinctive features, the most notable of which are their large, curved tusks, used for defense and digging. They also possess facial warts, which are actually thickened skin pads that offer protection during fights. Their bodies are covered with sparse bristly hair, and they often have a mane running along their spine. Warthogs are known for their habit of entering burrows backward, allowing them to defend themselves from predators with their tusks facing outward.

Warthogs are herbivorous, primarily feeding on grasses, roots, berries, and bark. They are well-adapted to dry habitats and can survive long periods without water, although they prefer to remain within range of water sources when possible.
